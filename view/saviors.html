NBA Saviors

For lack of a less dramatic term, I use savior to mean a player present during the championship run of the team that drafted him. Since it genuinely warms my heart when teams that "build through the draft" find success, I was curious to see what impact saviors have had on title runs throughout history. Interact with the below graphs to explore this impact, and read on below for more detail.

FAQ

Q: What is this chart supposed to tell me?

A: Taking a step back...teams can acquire players in 3 major ways:

  1. Drafting them out of college/high school
  2. Trading current players/assets to grab them from another team
  3. Signing them as a free agent (i.e. hiring them once their existing runs out, even if that contract was with another team)
Teams have the opportunity to mold the players they draft and help set them up for great individual success. And when all goes well, these home-grown, talented drafees can help the team that drafted them win a championship. I.e. they can be "saviors", in a sense, because they helped earn a ring for the team that chose them on day one.

So, does developing players through the draft earn teams championships? The above chart can tell us the extent to which championship teams rely on home-grown talent. There are 3 ways I imagined measuring this so-called reliance:

  1. Minutes %: Out of the 960 minutes that are played during an NBA game (48 minute game duration, times 5 players on the court at once), what percentage of those minutes were played by a savior?
  2. Win share %: Out of the playoff games that the championship team won, what % of those wins are attributable to saviors? ("attributable" in the win shares sense)
  3. Raw count: The raw number of saviors on the championship roster. I zone in on players that played at least 8 mins.
No matter which measurement method you consider, the interpretation is the same: tall bars in the chart indicate a high reliance on draftees.

Q: What conclusions can be drawn?

A: Here are some interesting things I picked up on from the above chart. Feel free to investigate on your own and share your findings.

  • The '15 Golden State Warriors (a popular example of building through the draft) had the highest savior minutes percentage (66.3%) in the playoffs since Jordan's '93 Bulls (84%).
  • On the flip side, the '04 Pistons found success outside of the draft (e.g. the entire core of Billups, Rip, 'Sheed, and Big Ben). They have the lowest savior mins percentage (24.2%) ever, and the lowest WS percentage (23.8%) since Wilt Chamberlain's 76ers (14.5%).
  • The '77 Blazers are a rare, interesting example of team with a large number of saviors that did not account for a relatively large number of wins. Watch their bar decrease as you go from to .
  • Use the line chart to zoom into the '80s (by clicking and dragging on the line chart area). The Celtics and Lakers traded championships, and although I am a Celts fan, I have to marvel at how the '80s Lakers built through the draft in that era, growing their arsenal of saviors. Their savior WS % increased with each title, peaking at 83.3%.
  • In the last decade, only 3 rookies have played 8+ mins per game for their championship team: Norris Cole of the '12 Heat, Glen Davis of the '08 Celtics, and Fabricio Oberto of the '07 Spurs.
